## 1. Rapid Diagnostic Tests, Laboratory-Based Immunoassay and Nucleic Acid Testing Strategies for Long-Acting Injectable Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 16 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *PLoS Medicine* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention · 🔴 Diagnostics |
| **PMID** | [41990088](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41990088/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1371/journal.pmed.1005030](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1005030) |

**Summary:** This WHO-supported systematic review (38 reports from 22 studies, 15 countries; primarily cabotegravir LAI-PrEP, n = 8,171 RCT participants) assessed whether HIV testing strategy affects clinical outcomes and programmatic scalability of long-acting injectable PrEP. Meta-analysis found no significant difference in HIV detection rates between rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) and nucleic acid testing (NAT) (OR 0.66, 95% CI 0.29–1.50). Importantly, RDTs delivered same-day results versus up to 7 days for lab-based tests, enabled treatment initiation one day sooner, and cost US$4 versus US$22 per test — without compromising negative predictive value (~100%) at initiation. The authors conclude that RDT-based strategies are sufficiently accurate and far more scalable, and that countries should adopt the new WHO guidance supporting RDTs to prevent testing from becoming a barrier to LAI-PrEP access as resources become constrained globally.

## 2. Virologic Suppression Among HIV-Positive Pregnant and Lactating Women Receiving Antiretroviral Therapy in Africa: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 13 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *PLoS ONE* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment · ⚪ Epidemiology |
| **PMID** | [41973701](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41973701/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1371/journal.pone.0346045](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0346045) |

**Summary:** This PRISMA-compliant meta-analysis pooled 55 studies (304,883 participants) examining virologic suppression (≤1,000 copies/mL) among HIV-positive pregnant and breastfeeding women across Africa. The pooled suppression rate was 80.86% (95% CI: 77.63–84.09%), falling significantly short of the global 95% target; undetectable viral load (<50 copies/mL) was achieved by only 60.92%. Key predictors of suppression included adherence to first-line ART (AOR 6.53, 95% CI 1.93–22.06), good drug adherence (AOR 3.61), and HIV status disclosure to a partner (AOR 1.66). Younger women (15–24 years) were at elevated risk of virologic failure (AOR 0.49). The findings highlight urgent need for integrated, targeted interventions in maternal HIV care to prevent vertical transmission and meet elimination targets.

## 3. Efficacy of Dolutegravir/Lamivudine in Adults with HIV-1 and Isolated Reactive Hepatitis B Core Antibody: Insights from Phase 3/3b Study Results

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 16 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *HIV Medicine* |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — Pooled Phase 3/3b RCT Analysis |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41992416](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41992416/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1111/hiv.70237](https://doi.org/10.1111/hiv.70237) |

**Summary:** This pooled analysis of five Phase 3/3b trials (GEMINI-1/-2, STAT, TANGO, SALSA; N = 2,798, of whom 76 had isolated reactive anti-HBc) evaluated safety and efficacy of the two-drug regimen dolutegravir/lamivudine (DTG/3TC) in a population where dual-drug ART is increasingly standard but co-morbidity data are scarce. In ART-naïve participants receiving DTG/3TC, 78% achieved HIV-1 RNA <50 copies/mL at Week 144 (GEMINI-1/-2); none of those who switched from 3DR/4DR to DTG/3TC experienced virologic rebound through Week 196 (TANGO). Zero cases of HBV reactivation occurred across all studies, and liver chemistry elevations were mild and infrequent. This analysis supports the safety and effectiveness of DTG/3TC as a treatment option for the substantial proportion of people with HIV who have prior HBV exposure, closing a critical evidence gap for two-drug regimens.

## 4. Characteristics of Small Airway Disease in Patients with HIV Infection: Insights from Spirometry and Impulse Oscillometry

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 15 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *BMJ Open Respiratory Research* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Cross-Sectional Study |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41985985](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41985985/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1136/bmjresp-2025-003719](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjresp-2025-003719) |

**Summary:** Among 127 people with HIV receiving ART in Istanbul (mean age 43.5 years; 60 non-smokers), this cross-sectional study found spirometric small airway disease (SAD) in 34% of participants, with impulse oscillometry (IOS) confirming significantly elevated R5, R5–R20, AX, and Fres values in those with SAD. Longer duration of ART and history of pneumonia were independently associated with SAD, underscoring that long-term ART use — while lifesaving — may contribute to subclinical respiratory morbidity. The optimal IOS cut-off for R5–R20 was 0.08 (sensitivity 71.8%, specificity 58.1%), suggesting IOS as a clinically useful supplemental diagnostic. Findings call attention to the pulmonary comorbidity burden in people aging with HIV on long-term antiretroviral regimens.

## 5. Assessing ChatGPT's Capability in Understanding and Reporting Antiretroviral Therapy Drug-Drug Interactions (ACCURATE-DDI)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 13 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *AIDS* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Cross-Sectional Observational Study |
| **Category** | 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [41968951](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41968951/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1097/QAD.0000000000004522](https://doi.org/10.1097/QAD.0000000000004522) |

**Summary:** Using ChatGPT-4o-mini, this multi-center pharmacy study tested 94 antiretroviral drug-drug interaction (DDI) pairs against validated HIV-specific DDI resources (HIV/HCV Drug Therapy Guide; Liverpool HIV Drug Interactions Checker). ChatGPT correctly classified only 40.4% of pairs, with sensitivity of 46.0% and specificity of 29.0%; the predominant error type was false negatives (60.7% of errors). Performance was especially poor for ritonavir interactions mediated through mechanisms other than CYP3A4 inhibition, and management guidance was frequently incorrect (mean score 0.9/2). The results indicate that clinicians should not rely on large language models as standalone tools for ARV DDI counseling, and highlight the need for AI validation frameworks before clinical integration of AI medication tools.

## 6. Characterizing AIDS Drug Assistance Program Practices and Policies for Sustained Viral Suppression Using the Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research: Protocol for a Qualitative Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 16 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *JMIR Research Protocols* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Study Protocol (Qualitative) |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [41858071](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41858071/) |
| **DOI** | [10.2196/90008](https://doi.org/10.2196/90008) |

**Summary:** This protocol paper describes an ongoing qualitative study using the Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR) to examine how operational, organizational, and contextual factors in AIDS Drug Assistance Programs (ADAPs) — which provide ART to low-income and uninsured Americans — drive variation in viral suppression rates across US states. Thirty-three individuals from 16 states (all four US census regions) were interviewed between February–March 2025; analysis is underway as of March 2026. ADAPs currently achieve an 85% national viral suppression rate, below the 90% End the HIV Epidemic target. Results are expected to identify policy, formulary, and operational levers that can close the remaining gap. The study is timely given ongoing debates about Ryan White HIV/AIDS Program funding levels and eligibility flexibility.

## 7. Unmet Patient Needs and Information Gaps in Fertility Counseling for Persons Living with HIV/AIDS: Evidence from Ghana

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 16 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *PLOS Global Public Health* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Qualitative Phenomenological Study |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [41990076](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41990076/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1371/journal.pgph.0006017](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgph.0006017) |

**Summary:** Twelve people living with HIV/AIDS (ages 25–45) attending an ART clinic in Ghana participated in in-depth semi-structured interviews examining fertility counseling experiences. Ninety-two percent reported minimal provider-initiated discussion of safer conception; 83% described insufficient counseling on contraceptive-ART interactions (including drug interactions with ART regimens and dual-protection methods); and all 12 participants (100%) were unaware of assisted reproductive technologies available to them. These results reveal a critical integration gap between reproductive health and HIV care services, even where ART is available and virologic suppression is achievable. Standardized provider training in contraceptive-ART pharmacology and contextualized counseling protocols are warranted, particularly in resource-limited sub-Saharan African settings.

## 8. Spatiotemporal Dynamics of HIV in the Brain: Perspective of Mathematical Modeling for Its Control via Antiretroviral Therapy

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 17 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Journal of Mathematical Biology* |
| **Study type** | Tier 5 — Mathematical Modeling Study |
| **Category** | 🟤 Basic Science · 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [41995870](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41995870/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1007/s00285-026-02382-6](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00285-026-02382-6) |

**Summary:** Researchers at San Diego State University and Chang Gung University developed a novel multi-compartment mathematical model to simulate spatiotemporal HIV dynamics across three brain regions — the prefrontal cortex, choroid plexus, and primary visual cortex — relevant to HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND). The model integrates drug pharmacodynamic parameters (dose, half-life, dose interval, dose-response slope) and the CNS Penetration-Effectiveness (CPE) score to derive an infection invasion threshold that predicts whether a given ART regimen will control HIV in the brain. Simulations demonstrate that CPE score and drug pharmacodynamics jointly determine brain viral control, enabling rational selection of ART combinations for patients with HAND risk. The work provides a computational framework to guide drug design and regimen optimization targeting the brain reservoir — a major obstacle to HIV cure.

## Key Themes

**1. Long-acting injectable PrEP is driving diagnostic innovation.** The WHO-backed PLoS Medicine systematic review demonstrates that RDT-based testing protocols for LAI-PrEP are clinically non-inferior to PCR/NAT approaches while being faster and dramatically cheaper. As cabotegravir and lenacapavir PrEP scale globally, testing protocol decisions will be as consequential as drug access.

**2. Two-drug regimens are proving robust across complex clinical scenarios.** The DTG/3TC pooled analysis in HIV Medicine adds important evidence for its safety in people with prior HBV exposure — historically a reason to favour three-drug regimens. Continued Phase 3 data collection is progressively closing the gaps that have made clinicians cautious about dual-therapy.

**3. Long-term ART success is generating a new cohort of aging comorbidities.** The BMJ Open Respiratory Research cross-sectional study and the mathematical brain dynamics model both signal that the next frontier in HIV care is managing long-term ART-associated organ damage — pulmonary and neurological — rather than virologic failure. Research on subclinical organ involvement in virally suppressed populations is accelerating.

**4. Implementation gaps remain the dominant barrier to the 95-95-95 goals.** The ADAP protocol (US), the virologic suppression meta-analysis in pregnant African women (80.86% vs. the 95% target), the fertility counseling study from Ghana, and even the AI DDI accuracy paper all converge on a single message: the drugs exist, but delivery systems, healthcare worker capacity, and patient-centred integration are lagging behind. Structural and programmatic research is as urgently needed as clinical pharmacology.
