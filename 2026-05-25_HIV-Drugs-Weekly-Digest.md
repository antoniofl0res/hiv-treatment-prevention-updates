## 1. Indirect Treatment Comparison of the Efficacy of Long-Acting Injectable Cabotegravir Compared With Lenacapavir for HIV Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 22 May 2026 — exact, verified |
| **Journal** | *Advances in Therapy* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Systematic review with indirect treatment comparison (ITC) |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [42171901](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42171901/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1007/s12325-026-03591-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12325-026-03591-7) |

**Summary:** In the absence of a head-to-head trial, Hawkins et al. conducted a network-based indirect treatment comparison (ITC) of long-acting injectable cabotegravir (CAB-LA, approved via HPTN 083/084) versus lenacapavir (LEN-LA, approved via PURPOSE 1/2) for HIV PrEP. Using two network approaches — one with no PrEP and one with daily oral TDF/FTC as the common comparator — and adjusting for differential adherence across trials, the analysis found comparable, high efficacy for both agents: predicted HIV prevention rates of 96–98% for CAB-LA in MSM/TGW and cisgender women, closely mirroring PURPOSE trial results for LEN-LA. Sensitivity analyses using both oral lead-in and injection-phase HPTN data confirmed robustness. The clinical implication is that formulary and access decisions between CAB-LA and LEN-LA should hinge on implementation factors — dosing frequency, supply chain, cost, and patient preference — rather than differential efficacy.

## 2. Long-Acting PrEP for People With High Vulnerability to HIV Acquisition in Brazil: A Cost-Effectiveness Analysis †

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | May 2026 — month verified; exact day unconfirmed (†marginal) |
| **Journal** | *Journal of the International AIDS Society* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Microsimulation cost-effectiveness model (CEPAC) |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention · 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42136105](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42136105/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1002/jia2.70116](https://doi.org/10.1002/jia2.70116) |

**Summary:** Chen et al. used the CEPAC microsimulation model to evaluate the clinical and economic impact of adding long-acting PrEP (CAB-LA or LEN-LA) to standard-of-care oral PrEP for MSM and transgender women (TGW) aged 18–49 in Brazil. Offering LA-PrEP alongside oral PrEP would reduce lifetime HIV risk from 21.4% to 16.8% in MSM and from 29.5% to 23.4% in TGW, with incremental life expectancy gains. Both CAB-LA and LEN-LA remain cost-effective at the Brazilian willingness-to-pay threshold of $8,740/YLS when priced below approximately $700–740/year. The analysis is most sensitive to LA-PrEP cost and HIV incidence, underscoring that negotiated pricing is the critical lever for equitable scale-up in high-incidence settings.

## 3. External Evaluation of Population Pharmacokinetic Models of Cabotegravir During Its Oral and Intramuscular Administration in HIV-Infected Patients †

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | June 2026 issue — epub date in May 2026 likely; exact online date unconfirmed (†marginal) |
| **Journal** | *CPT: Pharmacometrics & Systems Pharmacology* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Prospective multicenter observational PK study |
| **Category** | 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [42170807](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42170807/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1002/psp4.70180](https://doi.org/10.1002/psp4.70180) |

**Summary:** Renou et al. (ANRS0255 CARLAPOP study) externally validated two published population pharmacokinetic (popPK) models of cabotegravir — Han et al.'s trial-derived model and Thoueille et al.'s clinical-data model — using a prospective, independent dataset of 736 HIV-positive patients across 14 French centers (2,192 plasma concentrations). Neither model met the ≥70% prediction-within-20%-error threshold required for model-informed precision dosing (MIPD): Median Absolute Prediction Error was 17.9% (Han) and 15.3% (Thoueille) for IPRED, but global diagnostic failures were observed, including systematic bias in women and patients with higher BMI in Thoueille's model. The study concludes that current popPK models for cabotegravir are not yet reliable enough for routine clinical MIPD application and that refinement — particularly for sex and body-composition covariates — is needed before individualised dosing can be implemented.

## 4. Patient-Reported Quality of Life in People Living with HIV in Romania: A Cross-Sectional PROM-Based Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 21 May 2026 — exact, verified |
| **Journal** | *HIV Research & Clinical Practice* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Multicenter cross-sectional study |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42169383](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42169383/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1080/25787489.2026.2672255](https://doi.org/10.1080/25787489.2026.2672255) |

**Summary:** This large multicenter PROM-based study enrolled 1,042 adults attending routine HIV care across six Romanian centers (August 2024–July 2025), assessing eight quality-of-life domains using the OCTAVE instrument. Treatment-related wellbeing was very high: over 95% reported satisfaction with ART ease, adherence, tolerability, and information. However, significant gaps emerged in sexuality (63.1% positive), sleep quality (74.8%), and social/professional life (77.0%), with sexuality showing consistent age-related decline in older groups. Higher educational attainment was associated with better physical functioning, emotional wellbeing, and social integration. The study demonstrates that while ART optimisation has yielded high virological satisfaction, a substantial burden of unmet needs persists beyond viral suppression — particularly in sexual health and social participation — that require targeted, individualized interventions integrated into routine HIV care.

## 5. Perceptions of Young People Living with HIV and Caregivers in Botswana on Analytical Treatment Interruption Studies: A Qualitative Approach

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 20 May 2026 — exact, verified |
| **Journal** | *HIV Research & Clinical Practice* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Qualitative (purposive sampling, open-ended interviews) |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment · 📄 Review/Opinion |
| **PMID** | [42163437](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42163437/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1080/25787489.2026.2673739](https://doi.org/10.1080/25787489.2026.2673739) |

**Summary:** Koofhethile et al. conducted 32 in-depth interviews with young people living with HIV (median age 29), caregivers, healthcare providers, and community advocates in Gaborone and Palapye, Botswana, to assess knowledge and acceptability of analytical treatment interruption (ATI) studies as a component of HIV cure research. While all young people with HIV were unaware of ATIs and only a minority of caregivers and providers had prior knowledge, most participants deemed ATI research acceptable provided robust risk-mitigation measures were in place. The findings highlight a critical knowledge gap that must be addressed before ATI-inclusive cure trials can be ethically and practically scaled in high-burden African settings, with implications for consent processes, community literacy programs, and the design of participant safeguards.

## 6. Could AAV-Vectored Delivery of Broadly Neutralizing Antibodies (bNAbs) Reduce Virologic Failure in Children Diagnosed with Perinatal HIV?

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 22 May 2026 — exact, verified |
| **Journal** | *Human Vaccines & Immunotherapeutics* |
| **Study type** | Tier 5 — Opinion/Perspective (preclinical evidence synthesis) |
| **Category** | 🟤 Basic Science · 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [42171686](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42171686/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1080/21645515.2026.2655015](https://doi.org/10.1080/21645515.2026.2655015) |

**Summary:** Pringle and Martins argue that adeno-associated virus (AAV) vector-mediated delivery of HIV-1 broadly neutralizing antibodies (bNAbs) into skeletal muscle represents a scalable, long-acting alternative to repeated protein-biologic infusions for children with perinatal HIV in resource-limited settings. Citing rhesus macaque studies in which a single neonatal AAV administration produced multi-year bNAb expression sufficient to block simian-HIV in breastfeeding and sexual transmission models, the authors contend that ongoing SHIV-infected macaque studies examining ART-free virologic control justify clinical translation. The perspective highlights AAV-bNAb therapy as a potential functional cure adjunct or ART-bridging strategy for the approximately 1.4 million children living with HIV globally, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa where daily ART adherence and supply chain reliability remain persistent challenges.

## Key Themes

**1. Long-acting injectables are consolidating their clinical position.** The week's literature collectively reinforces that CAB-LA and LEN-LA have comparable, high efficacy for PrEP (ITC), while also surfacing pharmacokinetic limitations (cabotegravir popPK model inadequacy for precision dosing) and economic constraints (cost-effectiveness thresholds in Brazil). The field is shifting from efficacy demonstration to optimisation, accessibility, and real-world pharmacology.

**2. Implementation science is now the binding constraint.** Three of six articles address access, acceptability, or patient experience — LA-PrEP equity frameworks (referenced in WebSearch), pregnancy registry surveillance (near-window), cost-effectiveness modelling for Brazil, and QoL PROMs in routine Romanian care. Biomedical innovation has outpaced delivery systems, and the literature is responding accordingly.

**3. HIV cure research requires community investment.** The Botswana ATI qualitative study underscores that even among people living with HIV, awareness of cure research paradigms is minimal. Meaningful community engagement and structured literacy programs are prerequisites for ethical ATI trial conduct — particularly in high-burden African settings.

**4. Novel long-acting biologics (AAV-bNAbs) are emerging as a next-generation treatment frontier.** The bNAb perspective marks a conceptual shift beyond small-molecule ART toward gene-mediated passive immunization. If confirmed in ongoing macaque ART-discontinuation studies, this approach could offer a transformative durable-suppression strategy for pediatric HIV — a population with disproportionate adherence vulnerability.
