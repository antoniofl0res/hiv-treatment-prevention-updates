## 1. Vaccination Coverage Among Individuals Receiving HIV Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP): A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 24 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Preventive Medicine* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Systematic review & meta-analysis |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [42184839](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42184839/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/j.ypmed.2026.108611](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ypmed.2026.108611) |

**Summary:** This systematic review and meta-analysis (24 studies, 13,087 individuals; PRISMA guidelines, search through March 2026) quantified vaccination coverage among people receiving PrEP within routine care. HBV vaccination was highest at 73% (95% CI 59–88%), followed by mpox at 61% (40–83%) and HAV at 59% (48–71%), while HPV coverage was lowest at 43% (34–52%). High inter-study heterogeneity was observed and vaccination status was frequently self-reported. The review identifies substantial missed opportunities to integrate vaccination into PrEP care, calling for standardised approaches, proactive provider engagement, and economic evidence to support policy decisions.

## 2. Don't Drop the B: Risk of Hepatitis B Virus Infection and Reactivation in the Era of Tenofovir-Sparing Regimens for Treatment and Prevention of HIV

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 27 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Clinical Infectious Diseases* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Narrative review with clinical guidance |
| **Category** | 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [42200461](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42200461/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1093/cid/ciag322](https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciag322) |

**Summary:** This clinical review from Baylor, UCLA, and the University of Washington synthesises evidence on HBV risks posed by tenofovir-sparing ART and PrEP regimens—increasingly adopted for renal and bone safety reasons. Risk magnitude and mechanism vary by baseline HBV serology (susceptible, occult, resolved, active infection), and can include new infection or viral reactivation. The authors propose a practical clinical workflow for managing transitions away from tenofovir, including pre-switch HBV screening, vaccination, and post-switch monitoring. The key implication: HIV efficacy and patient convenience alone should not drive tenofovir-sparing decisions when HBV risk has not been addressed.

## 3. Genotypic Challenges in Implementing Broadly Neutralizing Antibody-Based Long-Acting HIV-1 Therapies

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 28 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Communications Medicine* (Nature portfolio) |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Review |
| **Category** | 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [42209804](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42209804/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1038/s43856-026-01680-w](https://doi.org/10.1038/s43856-026-01680-w) |

**Summary:** This review from Stanford, Minnesota, and the Ragon Institute addresses a key implementation challenge for next-generation long-acting regimens combining broadly neutralizing antibodies (bnAbs) with cabotegravir or lenacapavir. Accurate assessment of viral susceptibility to bnAbs is required before initiation, but phenotypic assays—the gold standard—lack scalability. The authors summarise in vivo and in vitro evidence linking HIV-1 Env sequence characteristics to reduced bnAb susceptibility, and conclude that genotypic methods can identify resistance in some viruses but cannot yet reliably predict susceptibility in clinical settings. Additional research on genotypic prediction is required before bnAb-based long-acting regimens can be widely deployed.

## 4. Navigating HIV-1 Drug Resistance: A Comprehensive Review of Protease Inhibitor Efficacy and Mutation Dynamics

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 29 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Drug Discovery Today* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Review |
| **Category** | 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [42217819](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42217819/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/j.drudis.2026.104711](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.drudis.2026.104711) |

**Summary:** This comprehensive review examines the mechanisms by which HIV-1 evolves resistance to protease inhibitors (PIs), with detailed analysis of mutation sites reducing PI susceptibility and their impact on long-term ART efficacy. It integrates current drug resistance testing methodologies—genotypic assays, phenotypic assays, and next-generation sequencing—and discusses emerging strategies for managing PI-resistant HIV-1, including use of darunavir/ritonavir combinations and optimised backbone regimens. As PI-based regimens remain critical for patients with INSTI or NRTI resistance, this review supports evidence-based guidance on when and how to preserve or rescue PI-based therapy.

## 5. Nurse Practitioner-Led, Same-Day Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis Delivery Program for People Who Inject Drugs in the United States: Findings From a Pilot Randomized Controlled Trial

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 27 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Journal of the Association of Nurses in AIDS Care (JANAC)* |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — Pilot randomized controlled trial |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [42201314](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42201314/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1097/JNC.0000000000000657](https://doi.org/10.1097/JNC.0000000000000657) |

**Summary:** This pilot RCT (n=44 HIV-seronegative people who inject drugs; 1:1 randomisation) tested iRaPID, a nurse practitioner-led same-day PrEP model delivered within a syringe services program. PrEP uptake at 30 days was significantly higher in iRaPID vs. treatment-as-usual (OR 5.40; 95% CI 1.37–21.26; p=0.016), but the advantage was not maintained at 90 days (OR 0.19; p=0.133). Acceptability was high (mean 4.2/5 at 90 days). The study supports the feasibility of nurse-led, same-day PrEP initiation in harm reduction settings while highlighting the critical need for enhanced retention strategies—a key gap for this historically underserved population.

## 6. HIV Drug Resistance and Treatment Success in the Republic of Congo: Implications for Optimized Treatment

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 29 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *HIV Medicine* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Cross-sectional point prevalence |
| **Category** | 🔴 Diagnostics |
| **PMID** | [42216480](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42216480/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1111/hiv.70261](https://doi.org/10.1111/hiv.70261) |

**Summary:** A cross-sectional study of 510 children, adolescents, and young adults on ART in Pointe-Noire, Republic of Congo, found that 34% had detectable viral loads (>40 copies/mL). Among 147 viremic samples successfully sequenced via next-generation sequencing, NRTI and NNRTI resistance mutations were highly prevalent—M184V in 54.1% and K103N in 40.8%—while resistance to integrase inhibitors and capsid inhibitors was absent. HIV subtype A1 predominated (21%). These findings demonstrate the urgent need to integrate HIVDR genotyping into routine clinical practice in the Republic of Congo, and underscore the importance of preserving INSTI-based regimens as future treatment options in resource-limited settings where NRTI/NNRTI failures are common.

## 7. Evaluating the Role of Pharmacists in Providing Care with Long-Acting Injectables for HIV and PrEP

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 26 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Journal of the American Pharmacists Association (JAPhA)* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Retrospective cohort (referral review) |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42202989](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42202989/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/j.japh.2026.103149](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.japh.2026.103149) |

**Summary:** Retrospective review of 1,103 referrals to a pharmacist-led long-acting injectable antiretroviral (LAI-ARV) program at five Ohio clinics, covering cabotegravir/rilpivirine (CAB/RPV) for HIV treatment and cabotegravir alone for PrEP. Of these, 893 (81%) were approved and 210 denied; CAB/RPV referrals had higher denial rates due to safety and efficacy concerns. Among approved referrals, 264 required at least one pharmacist clinical intervention addressing HBV risk, drug-drug interactions, or adherence barriers before initiation. The study demonstrates that pharmacist-led collaborative practice agreements are a high-value model for optimising LAI-ARV candidate selection and supporting safe implementation in community health settings.

|---|---|

| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Qualitative process evaluation |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42218458](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42218458/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1186/s12913-026-14827-1](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12913-026-14827-1) |

**Summary:** Qualitative process evaluation of the MONART Trial in KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa, which tested an upgraded TIER.net electronic ART database combined with a viral load (VL) champion model to improve monitoring and early detection of treatment failure. The findings are directly relevant to programmes addressing systemic gaps in VL monitoring that contribute to delayed identification of ART failure and increased drug resistance.

## 9. Epidemiological Impact of Prioritising Long-Acting Injectable Cabotegravir (CAB-LA) to MSM with Low PrEP Adherence in the Netherlands: A Mathematical Modelling Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 25 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Journal of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndromes* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Mathematical modelling |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [42184273](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42184273/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1097/QAI.0000000000003882](https://doi.org/10.1097/QAI.0000000000003882) |

**Summary:** A mathematical model of the Dutch MSM HIV epidemic calibrated to national data compared the epidemiological impact of providing CAB-LA (assumed 91% effectiveness; injection every 8 weeks) to oral PrEP users stratified by adherence and to non-PrEP users. Assuming 100% coverage, targeting CAB-LA to oral PrEP users with low adherence (<50% days covered; 18% real-world effectiveness) would avert 862 new HIV infections (19.7% of all MSM infections) over 25 years. Expanding to medium/high adherence users or non-PrEP users offered limited additional benefit. The modelling supports prioritising CAB-LA to adherence-challenged oral PrEP users as an efficient and cost-effective introduction strategy in settings moving toward a 2030 HIV elimination target.

## 10. PrEP Discontinuation Among Sex Workers: Gender Disparities and the Impact of Housing Instability in a Prospective Cohort Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 25 May 2026 |
| **Journal** | *International Journal of Infectious Diseases* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Prospective observational cohort |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [42190938](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42190938/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/j.ijid.2026.108825](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijid.2026.108825) |

**Summary:** Prospective cohort study of 189 HIV-negative sex workers (30% cisgender women, 34% cisgender men, 36% transgender women) initiating PrEP at a Madrid hospital clinic between November 2022 and March 2025. Overall PrEP discontinuation was high at 9.9 per 100 person-months, with a median retention of only 7 months; loss to follow-up accounted for 76.3% of discontinuations. Cisgender women had the highest discontinuation rate (20.8 per 100 person-months; median retention 3.8 months) compared with transgender women (aHR 0.30) and cisgender men (aHR 0.37). Housing instability was an independent risk factor for discontinuation (aHR 1.91; 95% CI 1.17–3.12). Hospital-based delivery models alone are insufficient for structurally vulnerable sex worker populations, requiring community-based, equity-focused approaches.

## Key Themes

**1. Long-acting injectable agents are reshaping both treatment and prevention—but implementation barriers remain substantial.** Four articles this week examined LAI agents (cabotegravir, rilpivirine, bnAbs), confirming that the science of LA-ART is maturing while pharmacy workflows, genotypic susceptibility testing for bnAbs, adherence gaps, and differential real-world effectiveness remain key programmatic challenges. Mathematical modelling now supports targeted rollout to low-adherence PrEP users as the highest-impact strategy.

**2. Tenofovir-sparing regimens require HBV co-management protocols.** As tenofovir-sparing ART and PrEP become more common, the risk of HBV reactivation or new infection is emerging as a preventable patient safety issue. The *Clinical Infectious Diseases* review and the pharmacist-led LAI program data both highlight that HBV screening, vaccination, and monitoring should be embedded into every switch decision.

**3. Structural determinants—housing, insurance, geography—are the dominant predictors of PrEP persistence.** Data from Spain (sex workers), the US (PWID, MSM, PrEP deserts), and China (MSM) converge on a consistent finding: individual motivation and clinical access are secondary to structural factors including housing instability, cost, and geographic proximity to services. Biomedical advances in PrEP will not close coverage gaps without equity-focused delivery models.

**4. Drug resistance surveillance gaps in sub-Saharan Africa and beyond pose long-term threats to ART programme durability.** High NRTI/NNRTI resistance rates among young people in the Republic of Congo (M184V 54%, K103N 41%)—in the absence of integrase or capsid inhibitor resistance—underscore both the importance of transitioning to dolutegravir-based regimens and the critical need for routine HIVDR genotyping in resource-limited settings before these newer drug classes are also compromised.
