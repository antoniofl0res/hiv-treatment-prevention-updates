## 1. Expansion of Stemlike HIV-Specific CD8 T Cells and Limited Viral Epitope Diversity Characterize Durable Posttreatment Control of HIV

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 22 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Science Translational Medicine* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Multi-cohort observational study |
| **Category** | 🟤 Basic Science |
| **PMID** | [42018665](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42018665/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1126/scitranslmed.aed0042](https://doi.org/10.1126/scitranslmed.aed0042) |

**Summary:** This multi-cohort study compared HIV-specific CD8 T cell responses and viral reservoir characteristics across posttreatment controllers (PTCs), posttreatment non-controllers (NCs), and spontaneous elite controllers (ECs). Durable viral suppression after ART cessation (>1 year) was strongly associated with two features: robust expansion of HIV-specific CD8 T cells displaying a stemlike memory phenotype (high TCF-1, low TOX expression), and a viral reservoir with limited epitope diversity concentrated in conserved autologous regions. PTCs showed readily cytolytic CD8 T cells targeting conserved reservoir epitopes, whereas mutationally constrained epitope targeting was primarily a feature of elite controllers. These findings identify immunological correlates of post-treatment control with direct implications for guiding HIV cure immunotherapy strategies.

## 2. A New Type of Nonsuppressible Viremia Produced by HIV-Infected Macrophage

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 22 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *The Journal of Infectious Diseases* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Mechanistic/descriptive study |
| **Category** | 🟤 Basic Science |
| **PMID** | [42018762](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42018762/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1093/infdis/jiag171](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiag171) |

**Summary:** Investigators at UNC Chapel Hill characterised a previously underappreciated mechanism of primary nonsuppressible viremia (NSV) in four people with advanced immunodeficiency who remained viraemic for one or more years despite ART adherence. Genomic sequencing showed that viruses circulating during ART were drug-sensitive, genetically diverse, macrophage-tropic, and not evolving — consistent with ongoing production from long-lived HIV-infected macrophages rather than from replicating or clonally expanded CD4 T cells (the mechanism of secondary NSV). Notably, *vpr* defects independently accumulated in macrophage-tropic lineages, suggesting vpr influences macrophage survival or viral output. These findings identify a distinct biological mechanism underlying some cases of treatment non-suppression and may inform targeted adjunctive strategies.

## 3. Twice-Daily Bictegravir During Rifampicin-Based Tuberculosis Therapy: A Pragmatic Alternative

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 20 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *HIV Medicine* |
| **Study type** | Tier 5 — Pharmacokinetic letter/commentary |
| **Category** | 🟣 Pharmacology |
| **PMID** | [42007964](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42007964/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1111/hiv.70245](https://doi.org/10.1111/hiv.70245) |

**Summary:** This letter from Hospital Universitario Fundación Alcorcón (Spain) explores dose-intensified bictegravir/emtricitabine/tenofovir alafenamide (B/F/TAF) twice daily as a pragmatic approach to managing HIV-TB coinfection when standard alternatives — twice-daily dolutegravir or rifabutin-based regimens — are not feasible or tolerated. Rifampicin strongly induces CYP3A4 and UGT1A1, substantially reducing bictegravir plasma concentrations, but emerging pharmacokinetic data suggest that dose intensification may partially compensate for this interaction. The authors synthesise current PK evidence and propose that twice-daily B/F/TAF may be a clinically reasonable option in selected patients, while acknowledging that prospective trial data are lacking. This is practically relevant given the high global burden of HIV-TB coinfection and tolerability challenges with dolutegravir.

## 4. Considerations for the Use of AAV-Based Gene Therapy in HIV-Positive Individuals With Haemophilia

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 25 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Haemophilia* |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Narrative/evidence review |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [42033288](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42033288/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1111/hae.70299](https://doi.org/10.1111/hae.70299) |

**Summary:** This guidance review by Rockstroh et al. addresses the practical intersection of adeno-associated virus (AAV)-based gene therapy for haemophilia and concurrent HIV management — a clinically relevant issue given historical HIV prevalence in the haemophilia community. Synthesising safety and efficacy data from gene therapy trial participants who were HIV-positive, the authors conclude that modern ART regimens do not appear to cause significant complications when combined with AAV-based gene therapies, and that HIV infection should not be considered a general contraindication. Key caveats include careful attention to hepatotoxic drug interactions, liver health monitoring, and individualised shared decision-making. The review provides a structured framework for clinicians navigating this emerging dual-treatment landscape.

## 5. Antiretroviral Therapy Persistence Among Treatment-Experienced People With HIV and Mental Health or Substance Use Disorders (USA, 2017–2024)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 23 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Journal of Comparative Effectiveness Research* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Retrospective cohort study |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42024232](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42024232/) |
| **DOI** | [10.57264/cer-2025-0124](https://doi.org/10.57264/cer-2025-0124) |

**Summary:** Using US commercial claims data (Optum Research Database, n=14,826 treatment-experienced PWH), this retrospective study compared 12-month persistence across six ART regimens in people with mental health disorders and/or substance use disorders (MHD/SUD) and in those with suboptimal adherence (PDC <85%). Among those on bictegravir/emtricitabine/tenofovir alafenamide (B/F/TAF), 12-month persistence was 80.3% (MHD/SUD group) and 78.6% (low adherence group), significantly higher than dolutegravir-containing regimens (DTG/3TC: 76.7%/70.8%; DTG/ABC/3TC: 68.9%/66.3%; DTG+F/TDF: 36.7%/41.9%). The findings suggest B/F/TAF may offer a persistence advantage in this high-risk subgroup, though Gilead Sciences-affiliated authorship warrants acknowledgement as a potential source of bias.

## 6. HIV Care Attrition Among Pregnant and Postpartum Adolescent Girls and Young Women in Tanzania

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 21 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *BMJ Open* |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Prospective cohort study |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42014142](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42014142/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1136/bmjopen-2025-107555](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjopen-2025-107555) |

**Summary:** This large prospective cohort (n=10,147 pregnant/postpartum adolescent girls and young women [AGYW] with HIV in Tanzania, 2018–2023) documented 3,259 attrition events across PMTCT services, with 79% occurring in the first year and a median time to first attrition of just 4 months. The attrition rate was more than double among AGYW newly started on ART at PMTCT enrolment versus those already on ART (28.6 vs 11.2 per 100 person-years). Risk was highest in those enrolled late in pregnancy and among the youngest adolescents (18–19 years). The study underscores critical retention failures in an already-vulnerable population and calls for targeted, repeated re-engagement interventions adapted to the cyclical nature of HIV care dropout among AGYW.

## 7. Acceptability of the Suubi+Adherence Intervention to Improve ART Adherence in Southern Uganda

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 24 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *PLOS ONE* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Qualitative study (nested in longitudinal RCT) |
| **Category** | 🟡 Implementation |
| **PMID** | [42030316](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42030316/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1371/journal.pone.0347112](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0347112) |

**Summary:** This qualitative sub-study (n=36 youth) analysed acceptability of the Suubi+Adherence intervention — a poverty-focused combination approach involving matched savings accounts, mentorship, and financial literacy training — among Ugandan adolescents living with HIV. Using the Theoretical Framework of Acceptability and thematic analysis, participants reported high motivation linked to economic learning opportunities and appreciated the Wisepill electronic monitoring device for medication reminders. Key barriers included travel distances and session timing conflicts. The findings demonstrate that economic empowerment interventions can meaningfully address structural drivers of poor ART adherence in youth, with policy implications for integrating financial support into HIV care programmes in sub-Saharan Africa.

## 8. Unintended Consequences of Life-Saving Pharmaceutical Innovations: How HAART Led to the Resurgence of Syphilis

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 22 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Health Economics* |
| **Study type** | Tier 4 — Quasi-experimental econometric analysis |
| **Category** | ⚪ Epidemiology |
| **PMID** | [42015679](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42015679/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1002/hec.70100](https://doi.org/10.1002/hec.70100) |

**Summary:** Exploiting geographic variation in pre-HAART AIDS prevalence as a natural experiment, this US study used a triple-differences framework to estimate the effect of HAART introduction (1996) on syphilis incidence through behavioural change. A one standard deviation increase in pre-HAART AIDS prevalence led to a 17.8% increase in syphilis incidence, and the model estimates 81% fewer syphilis cases between 1996 and 2008 in the counterfactual absence of HAART. The authors propose that transforming HIV from a terminal to a chronic condition reduced perceived risk of unprotected sex among both HIV-positive and HIV-negative individuals. While the analysis is observational and limited to historical US data, the findings are a timely reminder that next-generation long-acting preventive and treatment innovations may carry analogous unintended public health consequences requiring proactive mitigation.

## Key Themes

**1. HIV cure science is converging on immune T cell phenotype and reservoir structure.** Two mechanistically distinct papers this week — the Science Translational Medicine study on stemlike CD8 T cells in post-treatment controllers and the J Infect Dis study on macrophage-driven nonsuppressible viremia — highlight that durable control and treatment failure are both partly determined by the qualitative characteristics of immune responses and reservoir compartments, not just viral load. This has direct implications for cure trial design.

**2. Pharmacokinetic management of HIV-TB coinfection remains an unresolved clinical challenge.** The bictegravir letter underlines that rifampicin-based interactions continue to limit regimen flexibility in the high-burden settings where HIV-TB coinfection is most common. Prospective PK studies for dose-intensified integrase inhibitors are urgently needed.

**3. ART adherence and retention gaps are structural, not merely behavioural.** Three papers this week (Suubi+Adherence Uganda, Tanzania PMTCT cohort, US MHD/SUD persistence study) converge on a common message: adherence failures are driven by poverty, mental health burden, and systemic barriers, and are best addressed through combined clinical and social interventions with careful regimen selection for high-risk subgroups.

**4. Long-acting HIV prevention had no new peer-reviewed publications indexed this week.** Despite the rapid clinical adoption of twice-yearly lenacapavir and injectable cabotegravir as PrEP, no new prevention-specific studies appeared in PubMed within the 7-day window. This may reflect publication timing and indexing lag. Readers tracking this field should monitor NEJM, Lancet, and Nature Medicine directly, where recent high-profile trial data have been published in early 2026.
