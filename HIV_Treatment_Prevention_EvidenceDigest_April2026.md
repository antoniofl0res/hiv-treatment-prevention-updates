## 1. Switch to Single-Tablet Bictegravir–Lenacapavir from a Complex HIV Regimen (ARTISTRY-1): A Randomised, Open-Label, Phase 3 Clinical Trial

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 25 February 2026 |
| **Journal** | *The Lancet*, Vol. 407, Issue 10535, pp. 1249–1258 |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — Phase 3 Randomised Controlled Trial (multicenter, open-label, non-inferiority) |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41763229](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41763229/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/S0140-6736(26)00307-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(26)00307-7) |

**Summary:** ARTISTRY-1 enrolled 557 people living with HIV-1 who were virologically suppressed on complex multi-tablet regimens (median age 60 years; median HIV treatment duration 28 years, IQR 22–32), randomising 2:1 across 15 countries to switch to once-daily oral bictegravir–lenacapavir 75 mg/50 mg single-tablet regimen (STR) or to continue their complex regimen. At week 48, virological failure (HIV-1 RNA ≥50 copies/mL) occurred in just 1% of participants in each arm (difference −0.3%; 95% CI −2.3 to 1.8), demonstrating non-inferiority against the pre-specified 4% margin. No resistance mutations emerged in either arm. Adverse event rates and discontinuation rates were comparable. Participants switching to bictegravir–lenacapavir reported significantly increased treatment satisfaction, a clinically meaningful secondary outcome for a highly treatment-experienced population. This trial establishes bictegravir–lenacapavir STR as a viable simplification option for people unable to tolerate standard single-tablet regimens due to resistance, contraindications, or drug interactions.

## 2. Pembrolizumab in People With HIV Who Are Immunologic Nonresponders: A Phase 1 Randomised Controlled Trial

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 3 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Open Forum Infectious Diseases*, Vol. 13, Issue 4, pp. ofag203 |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — Phase 1 Randomised Placebo-Controlled Trial |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41983147](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41983147/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1093/ofid/ofag203](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofag203) |

**Summary:** This NIH-led phase 1 trial tested a single 200 mg dose of pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) versus placebo in seven people with HIV who were immunologic nonresponders — virally suppressed on ART for ≥12 months yet with persistently low CD4+ counts (100–350 cells/mm³). The primary safety endpoint was met: only one grade-3 event occurred (ophthalmic zoster in the placebo recipient), and no autoimmune events requiring corticosteroids were observed. Pembrolizumab sharply reduced PD-1 expression on both CD4+ T cells (from 60.4% to a nadir of 0.8%–23.5%) and CD8+ T cells (from 45.2% to 0.1%–23.2%), confirming target engagement. No significant changes in viral load or proviral DNA were observed at this single-dose level, suggesting that further dose-escalation or multi-dose regimens will be needed. This is an important early signal that checkpoint inhibition may safely modulate immune exhaustion in treatment-experienced PWH with suboptimal immune reconstitution.

## 3. Better Longitudinal Adherence to ART Among Virally Suppressed People With HIV Is Associated With Reduced Serious Non-AIDS Events

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 3 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Open Forum Infectious Diseases*, Vol. 13, Issue 4, pp. ofag196 |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Prospective Observational Cohort Study (ACTG A5001 / A5322) |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41994346](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41994346/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1093/ofid/ofag196](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofag196) |

**Summary:** Analysing deidentified data from 2,940 participants in the ACTG A5001/A5322 prospective studies (median follow-up 6.2 years), this study examined whether ART adherence above 90% — even in the context of viral suppression — further reduces the risk of serious non-AIDS events (SNAEs) including cardiovascular disease and death. Among 237 SNAE occurrences (103 deaths, 134 cardiovascular events), adherence >90% was associated with a 39% reduction in SNAE risk (aHR 0.612, 95% CI 0.390–0.961). Nearly all participants (95.9%) maintained viral suppression to <200 copies/mL throughout. The findings refute the intuition that viral suppression alone fully protects against non-AIDS morbidity, and reinforce the case for sustained high adherence even after achieving an undetectable viral load — with direct implications for adherence counselling programmes.

## 4. Initiation of Dolutegravir vs Efavirenz: Effect on Weight, BMI, Blood Pressure, and Incident Hypertension — A Target Trial Emulation

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 10 March 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Annals of Epidemiology*, Vol. 117, pp. 110074 |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Target Trial Emulation (prospective observational data, inverse probability weighting) |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41819297](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41819297/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/j.annepidem.2026.110074](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.annepidem.2026.110074) |

**Summary:** Using the Target Trial Emulation Framework with inverse probability weighting, this Boston University–Wits study emulated a comparative effectiveness trial among 2,930 treatment-naïve people initiating HIV treatment in Johannesburg, South Africa (2019–2022): 1,847 on dolutegravir and 1,083 on efavirenz. At 12 months, dolutegravir initiation was associated with +2.9 kg greater weight gain (95% CI −0.3 to 5.5), +3.9 mmHg greater systolic blood pressure increase (95% CI 1.2–6.6), and a 35% higher risk of incident hypertension (95% CI 0.04–0.5). These effects attenuated but persisted at 24 months. The study highlights important cardiometabolic trade-offs with dolutegravir-based first-line therapy in Sub-Saharan African populations and underscores the need for monitoring and management of weight and blood pressure in this group.

## 5. HIV Drug Resistance Among Children and Adolescents With Viraemia in Lesotho and Tanzania: Nested Analysis in the GIVE MOVE Trial

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 4 March 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Journal of Antimicrobial Chemotherapy*, Vol. 81, Issue 4 |
| **Study type** | Tier 3 — Nested Observational Analysis Within a Randomised Controlled Trial |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41793741](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41793741/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1093/jac/dkag070](https://doi.org/10.1093/jac/dkag070) |

**Summary:** This pre-planned analysis from the GIVE MOVE trial (NCT04233242) characterised drug resistance patterns in 137 children and adolescents (6 months to <19 years) with recent viraemia in Lesotho (77%) and Tanzania on ART. At the time of genotypic resistance testing, 58% were receiving protease inhibitor-based, 43% dolutegravir-based, and 7% efavirenz-based regimens. Despite persistent viraemia, 58% of participants still had three fully active drugs predicted in their current regimen — suggesting that resistance alone could not explain most treatment failures. Of 312 detected resistance-associated mutations, the majority targeted NNRTIs and NRTIs; only one participant had high-level dolutegravir resistance. The key implication is that non-adherence, not resistance, is the dominant driver of paediatric treatment failure in these settings, redirecting the clinical priority toward adherence support rather than automatic regimen switches.

## 6. Proteomic Profile After Intervention With Eplerenone Among Persons With HIV (MIRABELLA HIV Trial)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 8 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Open Forum Infectious Diseases*, Vol. 13, Issue 4, pp. ofag207 |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — Exploratory Substudyof a 12-Month Placebo-Controlled Randomised Trial |
| **Category** | 🔵 Treatment |
| **PMID** | [41994349](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41994349/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1093/ofid/ofag207](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofag207) |

**Summary:** Leveraging stored samples from the MIRABELLA HIV placebo-controlled trial (NCT02740179), this Massachusetts General Hospital / Harvard study used targeted proteomics (276 proteins via Olink) to investigate how eplerenone — a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist — reduces arterial inflammation in virally suppressed people with HIV. Eplerenone treatment was associated with significant reductions in eight proteins, including CDH1, FGF-21, and Gal-4, all of which correlated directly with changes in target-to-background ratio on cardiac PET/CT (ρ = 0.53–0.64, p < 0.01). Two proteins (CSTB, MPO) increased with eplerenone, and the inflammatory rise of IL-27 was prevented. These proteomic signatures provide mechanistic insight into how eplerenone may interrupt pathways of arterial inflammation — epithelial barrier disruption, vascular dysfunction, and metabolic dysregulation — and strengthen the rationale for exploring aldosterone antagonism as a cardiovascular risk reduction strategy in people with HIV.

## 7. Lenacapavir as Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis for HIV Prevention (Cochrane Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 1 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews*, Vol. 4, pp. CD016347 |
| **Study type** | Tier 1 — Cochrane Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (PROSPERO: CRD420251080791) |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [41919720](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41919720/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1002/14651858.CD016347](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD016347) |

**Summary:** This Cochrane review — part-funded by the South African National Department of Health — synthesised data from two RCTs (PURPOSE 1 and PURPOSE 2) enrolling 8,660 participants across South Africa, Uganda, USA, Brazil, Thailand, Peru, Argentina, and Mexico. Twice-yearly subcutaneous lenacapavir demonstrated a large reduction in new HIV infections at 52 weeks compared to oral PrEP (RR 0.07, 95% CI 0.02–0.22; high-certainty evidence), translating to 14 fewer infections per 1,000 people treated, with an NNTB of 70. Lenacapavir also slightly reduced serious adverse events (RR 0.78; NNTB 128) with no difference in all-cause mortality, though injection site reactions were more frequent (RR 1.68; NNTH of 4). No trials compared lenacapavir to injectable cabotegravir or placebo. The review provides the strongest available evidence synthesis on lenacapavir PrEP and offers the evidentiary foundation for national guideline development and procurement decisions globally.

## 8. Rapid Diagnostic Tests, Laboratory-Based Immunoassay, and Nucleic Acid Testing Strategies for Long-Acting Injectable PrEP: A Systematic Review

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 16 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *PLoS Medicine*, Vol. 23, Issue 4, pp. e1005030 |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — WHO-Supported Systematic Review (GRADE methodology; PROSPERO: CRD42024605562) |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [41990088](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41990088/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1371/journal.pmed.1005030](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1005030) |

**Summary:** This WHO-supported systematic review of 38 reports from 22 studies (across 15 countries) compared rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs), laboratory immunoassays, and nucleic acid testing (NAT) for HIV screening in the context of long-acting injectable PrEP (cabotegravir: 20 studies; lenacapavir: 2 studies). Among 8,171 LAI-PrEP users in four RCTs, HIV detection rates were statistically equivalent between RDT and NAT strategies (OR 0.66, 95% CI 0.29–1.50, p = 0.87). RDTs enabled same-day turnaround and treatment initiation versus up to 7 days and 6–9 days for laboratory-based approaches, at a fraction of the cost (US$4 vs $22 per test). Critically, delayed HIV detection was rare with both strategies (11/8,171 for RDT vs 0/8,171 for NAT), while NAT occasionally produced false positives leading to unnecessary PrEP holds (7/2,483 in HPTN 083). The review supports WHO guidance recommending RDT-based strategies as the standard for LAI-PrEP delivery, removing a key implementation barrier in resource-limited settings.

## 9. Enhanced Peer PrEP Referral With HIV Self-Testing Delivery Among Young Kenyan Women: A Randomised Controlled Trial of Peer Networks

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 30 March 2026 |
| **Journal** | *PLoS Medicine*, Vol. 23, Issue 3, pp. e1005023 |
| **Study type** | Tier 2 — Randomised Controlled Trial |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [41911315](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41911315/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1371/journal.pmed.1005023](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1005023) |

**Summary:** This RCT (NCT04982250) conducted in Central Kenya randomised 82 PrEP-experienced index peers aged 16–24 to enhanced peer referral (group training plus delivery of 8 HIV self-testing kits) or standard informal referral, tracking outcomes for 241 referred young women. Despite high acceptability and feasibility, enhanced peer referral did not significantly increase PrEP initiation (30% enhanced vs 41% standard; RD −6%, 95% CI −26% to 11%; p = 0.51) or PrEP continuation. However, it was strongly associated with a 39 percentage-point increase in recent HIV testing among referred peers (95% CI 24–54%; p < 0.001) — a critical gateway to prevention. The intervention cost nearly 8× more per peer referred to PrEP ($23 vs $3). Three referred peers, all in the enhanced arm, tested HIV-positive. The results suggest that peer-delivered self-testing alone is insufficient to bridge the PrEP initiation gap but may be usefully combined with additional structural supports — informing how community-based delivery models are designed for adolescent girls and young women in high-burden settings.

## 10. Targeted Universalism for Long-Acting PrEP: An Urgent Need to Avoid Risk Targeting and Build Population-Level Impact

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Published** | 8 April 2026 |
| **Journal** | *The Lancet HIV* (online first) |
| **Study type** | Tier 5 — Expert Policy Review |
| **Category** | 🟢 Prevention |
| **PMID** | [41965241](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41965241/) |
| **DOI** | [10.1016/S2352-3018(26)00034-2](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2352-3018(26)00034-2) |

**Summary:** This Lancet HIV commentary from Johns Hopkins and partner institutions argues that the rollout of new long-acting prevention products — lenacapavir, cabotegravir, and the investigational monthly oral MK-8527 — risks repeating the access failures of oral PrEP if launch decisions continue to rely on epidemiologically-driven risk targeting. The authors propose "targeted universalism" as a policy framework: setting universal access as the goal, while tailoring functional supports (convenient delivery points, provider training, stigma reduction) to remove barriers for the most underserved groups. The critique is empirical: oral PrEP has reached only 16.5% of the UNAIDS 2025 target globally, in part because risk-based targeting concentrated PrEP in clinical settings that excluded structurally marginalised populations. As lenacapavir PrEP scales up following FDA approval and its Cochrane evidence review, this framework offers a direct policy blueprint. While a commentary rather than a primary study, its publication in The Lancet HIV at this particular inflection point gives it significant agenda-setting weight.

## Key Themes

**1. Lenacapavir's dual dominance — treatment and prevention.** The ARTISTRY-1 Lancet trial and the Cochrane lenacapavir PrEP review published weeks apart confirm lenacapavir as the decade's most consequential HIV molecule: capable both of simplifying complex treatment regimens as a single-tablet STR and of reducing new infections by 93% when used as twice-yearly PrEP.

**2. The adherence paradox in virally suppressed patients.** Two distinct studies — the ACTG cohort on serious non-AIDS events and the GIVE MOVE paediatric resistance analysis — converge on the same message: viral suppression is necessary but not sufficient. In adults, adherence >90% independently cuts cardiovascular risk and mortality even at undetectable viral loads. In children, poor adherence rather than resistance explains most virological failures.

**3. Long-acting PrEP requires systemic delivery redesign.** The WHO-supported LAI-PrEP diagnostics review and the targeted universalism commentary together make clear that injectable PrEP cannot be distributed through existing oral PrEP infrastructure without modification. RDT-based testing removes a key bottleneck; universalism-oriented roll-out frameworks are needed to avoid replicating oral PrEP's coverage failures.

**4. HIV as a cardiometabolic disease.** The dolutegravir hypertension study and the eplerenone proteomics trial reflect a growing research paradigm: for people on effective ART, cardiovascular risk — not opportunistic infection — is now the primary clinical challenge. Both studies point toward treatment regimen optimisation and aldosterone antagonism as emerging management strategies for a maturing epidemic.
